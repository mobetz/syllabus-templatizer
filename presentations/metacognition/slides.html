What is a Neuron?

What is a neuron?

A neuron is a cell in your brain that acts as an information messenger.

These cells use a pulse of electricity (and some salts) to help your thinking jump between related ideas.

What is a Neuron?

Clusters of neurons:

  • record memories
  • process sensory inputs (like sight and hearing)
  • represent ideas and knowledge

Your brain is really just a large spiderweb of connected neurons.

When two ideas become connected in your mind, the neurons representing those ideas stretch and grow, building an electrical path between those parts of your brain!

Making Connections

When we think about a topic, our attention is like a small spark that can travel along these neurons.

The process that governs how this spark moves is called our executive function.

Information Retrieval

When we try to retrieve a memory, your spark has to jump from idea to idea to 'find' it.

This is why sometimes a phrase or even a familiar smell or taste might 'trigger' a related memory.

Information Retrieval

Sometimes, your spark might have to travel a long way to get from one idea to another.

If you travel this pathway often enough, your brain might start looking for a shortcut to quickly jump between these concepts...

Information Retrieval

It does this by reinforcing a new neural pathway!

This is how learning is physically represented in your brain.

These connections can be any length -- your 'spinal cord' is a bundle of very long neurons connecting ideas about how to move to the muscles throughout your body.

Information Retrieval

We said learning happens when we travel a pathway intensely or often...

What do you think happens if we add Google to the mix?

Information Retrieval

When we rely too much on Google, software tools, or copying answers, our brain "finds" the information at that location, and so that is the only connection we make.

This is a phenomenon called transactive memory -- your brain likes to save space, and it is much easier to remember where it can find an answer instead of learning all the related ideas.

Memory Encoding

We've talked about 'finding' related information, but what about storing a completely new idea?

This is a process called encoding a memory, and it works similarly to retrieval!

Memory Encoding

When you identify a new concept, your brain reviews all the ideas you've been focused on.

We can think about this like keeping a 'history' of all the places your spark has traveled recently.

Memory Encoding

When you encode that new concept, your brain will connect that idea to all those recently traveled pathways.

This is the theory behind active listening -- if you are trying to learn something new, regularly pausing and thinking about how new information is related to previously learned concepts ensures you get a good network of 'starter' connections.

Memory Encoding

Humans are really bad at focusing on multiple things at once...

Even if you think you are multi-tasking, your brain's "history" of its spark is going to look more like this.

Memory Encoding

When it tries to encode the information, it won't have any connections to make!

You might get a loose memory that an idea was covered in a particular lecture, but it won't be connected to any other ideas, so you won't be able to retrieve it.

The Role of Sleep and Healthy Diet in Learning

As you concentrate, your 'spark' causes damage. Your body needs time to repair neurons and build back stronger (just like how working out causes tears and makes you feel sore, but gives you stronger muscles after rest.)

During sleep, your brain has its best chance to repair and refresh your neurons. If you are tired, it likely means your body hasn't had a chance to make repairs!

Sleep also gives your 'spark' a chance to explore. This is one theory behind why we dream -- Dreaming is your mind traveling pathways it might not while you are awake, forming connections and helping you learn.

The Role of Sleep and Healthy Diet in Learning

Your brain needs protein, fat, energy, and nutrients as building blocks to create neurons, and to make repairs.

If you try to learn while hungry, you will be in 'energy-saving' mode; your body will create the fewest connections it thinks it needs.

If you never use something you learned, your body will try to conserve energy by letting that connection go unmaintained, making it harder to use. We practice with labs and homework so your brain knows that connection is important!

Effective Studying

Studies suggest we can really only encode information in ten to twenty minute bursts.

If you try to learn longer than this without a reset, you will only remember things at the beginning and end. This is called the Primacy and Recency Effect.

What does this mean for studying?

If you try to cram for a test or marathon an entire lab in one day, you will only remember a small amount of the work you did!

The Metacognitive Model

So how can we use all this information about our brains to help us succeed?

We can use this information to monitor ourselves as we learn and regulate our behaviors.

Self-regulated learning happens in a cycle of three stages:

  1. Forethought - Set a goal, then schedule and plan how to achieve that goal.
  2. Performance - Implement your plan while monitoring performance.
  3. Self-Reflection - Think back on records of your performance and evaluate successes/failures.

The Metacognitive Model

Computer programming works the same way! We should always plan before we code, then reflect on what parts of the program work or don't work.

Note: This is why I always start lectures with learning outcomes that represent our "goals" -- so you can reflect on how each new idea I introduce relates to these goals.

This way, our brain forms the right connections to make these problems easier in the future!
